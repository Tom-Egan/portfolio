L560 Range Rover Velar

Project Snapshot (TL;DR)

Overview

  • Project: Range Rover Velar 24MY Infotainment Update
  • Role: UX Designer
  • Team: Two UX Designers, UX Researchers, Product Engineering Management
  • Timeframe: 2020-2021
  • Platform: Pivi/Pivi Pro (embedded automotive infotainment)

Outcomes

  • Replaced physical controls with touchscreen interactions
  • Shipped on 2024 Range Rover Velar
  • Interaction patterns scaled across all JLR vehicles
  • Met legal, safety, and development requirements

Summary

///

For the 24MY (2024 Model Year) Range Rover Velar mid-cycle refresh, Jaguar Land Rover removed a significant number of physical controls as part of a redesigned interior strategy. This resulted in several mission-critical vehicle functions needing to be safely and intuitively relocated into the touchscreen.

As one of UX designers, I worked with a small design team to re-architect the existing Pivi infotainment system, adhering to strict legal, functional, and development constraints. Through multiple design interations, interactive prototypes, and user tests, we delivered a production-ready solution that launched on the Velar and later scaled across all JLR vehicles for 24MY and beyond.

2024 Range Rover Velar interior

Objective

///

The objective was to provide quick access to the high-frequency, safety-critical controls (previously operated by physical buttons) into the Pivi touchscreen. Affected features included:

  • Climate/HVAC
  • Heated/cooled seats
  • Volume
  • Drive modes
  • Defrost
  • Miscellaneous settings (e.g. Auto Start/Stop)

Design Constraints

///

The primary design challenge was that the sandbox we were given to design a solution in, was limited to the system sidebars and system overlays. This constraint came from points of functional requirements, legal requirements, and software development—defining the solution space and limiting our design flexibility.

  • Functional: Mission-critical systems such as climate and drive modes needed to be globally accessible in the system (i.e. can be accessed at anytime, regardless of which screen the user is on). In certain system states (e.g. while Apple CarPlay or Android Auto is active), the sidebars would be the only global entry point for these features.
  • Legal: Functions such as Rear Defrost came with their own set of rules which needed to be considered and also dictated their implementation.
  • Development: Integrating brand new, system level interactions into an existing software architecture meant that time and cost to develop factored into how much the system could/should be modified.

Design Approach

///

The What

A straightforward goal: Provide quick access to these high priority use cases, formerly physical controls.

The Who

The concept work was split up between a team of two designers, Mutlu Isik and myself, where we decided to tackle individual feature sets, one at a time before piecing it together into a larger interaction model. Reason being, this would be the first time these functions would be performed on the touchscreen in JLR vehicles - resulting in a lot of individual nuance to sort out (e.g. solving the temperature control interactions, then solving the broader layout of the whole climate screen).

The How

  1. Design: Two, contrasting concepts created in parallel (in Sketch/Figma)
  2. Develop: High-fidelity, interactive prototypes of the design direction(s) created (in Proto.io)
  3. User Test Plan: Coordinate with the UX Research team
    • Determine interactions/user flows to examine
    • Define the user test tasks and user test plan
    • Conduct remote, A/B tests of the two prototypes
  4. User Test Debrief: Review with our the UX Research team, sharing insights, recommendations, and next steps
  5. Iterate: Take the research recommendations and insights and return to Step 1 with a refined design direction

Individual Input

///

I was responsible for end-to-end concept development, which included: wireframing, interaction design, high-fidelity prototyping, and research collaboration. I helped develop each of interactive prototypes used in all user tests and worked directly with UX Research teams in both Portland and the UK to define user test plans and refine designs post-testing.

With the JLR headquarters located in England, and our satellite office in Portland, Oregon, this was a global effort that involved regular collaboration with management and stakeholders in the UK.

Solutions

///

Problem Space

Starting first with the driver sidebar layout—after accounting for the placement of Rear Defrost and Max Defrost (legal requirements), many back-and-forth dicussions of which features to prioritize unfolded. It became a matter of, the number of elements that needed to be added, exceeded the available space.

01

Contextual Buttons

  • Problem: Available space in the sidebar was insufficient for all required controls.
  • Solution: Introduced context-dependent buttons based on system state.
  • Outcome: Enabled access to four high-priority features using only two sidebar positions, without increasing interaction depth.

02

One solution to this problem was to introduce the idea of context-dependent buttons. Meaning, elements would change depending on the context or screen the user is on. For example, in the first version of Pivi for 20MY-23MY, there were two separate buttons for Home and Apps. With space being at an all-time premium in the 24MY update, those two buttons were merged together into one contextual button:

  • If the user is on the home screen, display a shortcut button to open the App Drawer.
  • If the user is not on the home screen, display a shortcut button to return Home.

Note: The App Drawer could still be accessed via swiping from the left edge as a secondary method (Not quite as discoverable as the dedicated button, but still an available option for the user).

This same idea was also applied to the Phone shortcut. As one of the deprioritized features, Phone no longer had a permanent shortcut in the sidebar. Some might ask, "but what if the user is on a phone call?" Similarly, in that context of being on an active phone call, the Media button would temporarily change to a shortcut to return to the active call screen. Given that the phone call audio would be coming through the cabin speakers, this made access to Media unncessary at that point in time—i.e. show the user the right thing at the right time. With this approach we were effectively able to serve up shortcuts to four features in two areas, freeing up two available slots in the sidebar.

Note: This same concept would also apply in the scenarios when CarPlay or Android Auto is connected, as they now become the media source.

Combo Defrost Button

  • Problem: Available space in the sidebar was insufficient for all required controls, one-touch activation of Rear Defrost legally required.
  • Solution: Combine two related functions into a single switch, toggling between multiple modes.
  • Outcome: Met legal requirement of one-touch activation of Rear Defrost, gained ability to turn two functions on in one press.

03

Another example of where creative problem solving became necessary was with the case of Rear Defrost and Heated Windshield. Functionally, these two features are related, in the sense that they both are turning on some sort of heated element in the glass. Given that, and the limited space in the sidebar, these two features seemed like logical candidates to pair together into one button. However, the legal requirement that was given to us—at any point, Rear Defrost must be able to be turned on within one press on the screen—presented a different challenge.

After several different design concepts and user tests, the final result was a toggle switch that cycles through four different states, while still satisfying the Rear Defrost legal requirement. Doing so also gave us the benefit of saving an interaction—the ability to turn two controls on at once, in a single button press.

Additional design notes—Because of the visual similarity between the two icons, F (front) and R (rear) indicators were added next to the icons. Visual reinforcement in the form of dedicated status indicators (dots) were added as well, so that the user could better differentiate which of the two is turned on and differentiate the button's intent (i.e. it now looks different than the Navigation shortcut button, so the user should expect it to behave differently). This combo button is also dependent on vehicle trim level, if Heated Windshield is not equipped then this becomes a simple on/off button for Rear Defrost.

Overlays

  • Problem: Broader feature sets needed quick access and to be globally accessible from any screen.
  • Solution: Use sidebar buttons as entry points to open an overlay, to allow for full-screen display.
  • Outcome: Quick access to a wider suite of climate, volume, and driving controls assessible from any screen (e.g. even while CarPlay is active).

04

Accessed from the sidebars, overlays were used to accommdate more complex and deeper feature sets, such as Climate and Drive Modes. This however, created it's own set of design challenges as now there was less consistency with how the sidebars behave—it became a mixed bag of interactions. In other words: certain buttons performed an on/off action, some buttons exit your current screen and take you somewhere new, and some keep you in the same context but display an overlay on top. It's debatable if that is actually a problem in itself. However, the problem occurs when it breaks the mental model of: things that look similar, should function similar (especially when grouped in close proximity).

To assist the user in identify the different intents of these buttons, or at least, tip the user off that if you press this, something different will happen, specific visual styling was applied to the overlay buttons. This was done for two reason: One, to indicate to the user that these buttons behave differently that the rest of the sidebar buttons. Two, the button could be repurposed, changing context to a close button once the overlay was opened.

Once an overlay is launched, the sidebars change to a more focused, empty state, removing unnecessary visual clutter. This was done to help communicate the context switch of having the overlay open, as well as provide the user a clearer visual path to their exit route (close button). One caveat to this stategy comes back to the previously mentioned legal requirements. Since one-touch activation of Rear Defrost and Max Defrost are required at all times, those two buttons needed to remain in place.

The Quick Slider

  • Problem: The new system lacked the ability for quick, large scale adjustments and quickly reach min/max values.
  • Solution: Add a vertical drag interaction to select sidebar buttons.
  • Outcome: Repurposed select sidebar buttons as draggable elements, maximizing our design sandbox.

05

Maximizing our design sandbox and to enable "no-look" or muscle-memory interactions while driving, secondary interactions were designed into the overlay buttons. In addition to pressing the buttons (to open an overlay), a drag interaction was added for quick and wide-band adjustments. With the physical volume control removed and now living in the touchscreen, having the ability to make quick (and large) adjustments to the volume level became one use case that benefitted from this interaction.

The sidebar background provides blue feedback for additional confirmation when the selected temperature is in the lower/colder ranges. The sidebar background provides red feedback for additional confirmation when the selected temperature is in the upper/warmer ranges. [Clip from "2025 Range Rover Controls Explained — Dashboard, Steering Wheel & Screen Guide" by MikesCarInfo (2025), YouTube, 6:54-7:05]

To promote the discoverability of the drag interaction, a deliberate visual element and affordance was added to the button: The Rail. A line extending outwards from the button was used to create the impression of the button sitting on a track, appearing more as a draggable element. The Rail also provided an opportunity communicate interaction intent with the temperature slider. With the blue tip used to suggest dragging down will lower the temperature, and the red tip, used to suggest a dragging up will raise the temperature. This same dragging interaction from the sidebar was also applied to the Drive Modes and Volume buttons as well.

The Preflight Checklist

  • Problem: Quick, top-level access to common functions upon entering the vehicle was now lost.
  • Solution: Once the system boots, surface a dedicated space where the user can access features they might regularly turn on/off when first getting into the vehicle.
  • Outcome: A new tile was added to the home screen that presented several controls all in one place.

06

To reduce cognitive load and add a quality-of-life improvement, a new tile was added to the home screen. This new tile presented the highest priority use cases, all in one spot once the system booted. The primary goal behind this was to help ease the transition from physical controls to touchscreen by surfacing those frequently used functions at the top level.

Results

///

Summary

  • Shipped as part of the 24MY mid-cycle refresh, debuting in the 2024 Range Rover Velar
  • Design solution rolled out across all JLR vehicles
  • Established new interaction patterns for all JLR vehicles
  • Met legal requirements for safety-critical functions
  • Designs validated through multiple rounds of user testing in Portland and the UK

06

Climate Overlay

The Climate Overlay is broken up into three sections of controls: temperature, heated/cooled seats, and fan speed. Several different layouts and individual control styles were explored and tested, including concepts where all three sections reused the exact same format of controls (e.g. temperature, heated seats, and fan speed all using up/down chevron controls). In those cases, the visual duplication and repetition became too much. In the end, each section was given control schemes that suited their use cases best. The visual variance of controls between sections enabled users quicker task completions.

[Screenshot from "2025 Range Rover Controls Explained — Dashboard, Steering Wheel & Screen Guide" by MikesCarInfo (2025), YouTube, 8:46]

For temperature, a variety of controls were provided to cover a range of use cases: small, medium, and large adjustments. (1) The single chevrons adjust the temperature up/down by one degree (0.5 degrees Celsius). (2) Double chevrons adjust in larger chunks, changing the temperature up/down in four-degree increments (two degrees Celsius). (3) Dedicated shortcuts to jump to the highest and lowest temperatures round out the suite of controls. Supplemental interactions were also included: press-and-hold any of the chevrons for continunous adjustment, and dragging up/down on the entire temperature area.

[Screenshot from "2025 Range Rover Controls Explained — Dashboard, Steering Wheel & Screen Guide" by MikesCarInfo (2025), YouTube, 8:46]

Moving over to Seats, this section is a small slice of a larger Seats app, providing quick access to the highest priority use case: seat heating/cooling. With a limited range of adjustments, we were able to display direct selection of high, medium, and low all at once, reducing the number of steps for the user. The visual centerpiece of the overlay, the seat graphic doubles as an interactive element - controlling which zone(s) heating/cooling is applied to. Finally, to increase user mobility around the system, a shortcut to the full Seats app (which includes additional features such as Massage) anchors the top edge of the screen.

The final section of the Climate Overlay contains the fan speed controls. Placement of this section in the overlay was very deliberate. Being the only "neutral" or shared control, it serves as a natural divider and visual break when both the driver and passsenger Climate Overlays are open at the same time. Interactions for fan speed are fairly straightforward: direct selection via press or dragging. Similarly to Seats, a shortcut to the wider Climate app is provided above.
